EOS — Entrepreneurial Operating System

PHG's operating framework and leadership gospel. EOS isn't a software system — it's how every meeting runs, how priorities get set, and how the company moves. Understanding EOS fluently is as important as knowing the tech stack.

The 6 Key Components
👁
1. Vision

Everyone in the company sees the same destination and knows the path to get there. Captured in the V/TO — PHG's one-page strategic plan: Core Values, Core Focus, 10-Year Target, Marketing Strategy, 3-Year Picture, 1-Year Plan, and Rocks.

2. People

3. Data

The Scorecard: 5–15 weekly numbers that give an absolute pulse on the business. Numbers don't lie. No opinions, no feelings — if the number is on track, it's green. If not, it's red and goes on the Issues list.

The Essential EOS Tools
🪨 Rocks — 90-Day Priorities

Rocks are the 3–7 most important things each person or team must accomplish in the next 90 days. They're specific, measurable, and assigned to one person. At the end of the quarter: done or not done. No gray area.

For Jimmy's onboarding: "Complete NCR CFC training to competency" or "Document 5 approved SOPs" would be example Rocks for Q3.
▾
What makes a good Rock?
S Specific — not "improve systems," but "complete R365 recipe costing audit for all 12 locations"
M Measurable — you can declare it done or not done, no ambiguity
A Attainable — meaningful but finishable in 90 days
R Realistic — you own it and have the ability to complete it
T Time-bound — due by end of quarter, not someday
Possible Q3 Rocks for Jimmy
→ Complete NCR CFC training to independent operation
→ Document and get Bill's approval on 5 SOPs
→ Complete R365 deep-dive training across all active modules
→ Present 90-day systems improvement roadmap to ownership
⏱ L10 Meeting — The Weekly Rhythm

Every leadership team meets weekly for exactly 90 minutes using this fixed agenda. Always the same format. The "10" stands for "Level 10" — the goal is to make every meeting a 10 out of 10.

5 min  Segue / good news
5 min  Scorecard review
5 min  Rock review (on track / off track)
5 min  Customer / employee headlines
5 min  To-do list (7-day tasks, done or not)
60 min  IDS — Issues Solving
5 min  Conclude / rate the meeting
▾
The rules that make L10 work
→ Same day, same time, every week. Non-negotiable. If you skip it, you lose momentum.
→ Start on time, end on time. Respecting the clock signals that everyone's time matters.
→ The to-do list is 7-day tasks only. Not projects, not goals — specific things due this week.
→ IDS gets 60 minutes because that's where the value is. Issues don't get tabled, they get solved.
→ Meeting rating at the end is real. If it's below 8, you say why — and fix it next week.
Ask Bill: When is PHG's L10? Who's in the room? What's consistently on the Issues list? How long has the format been running?
📈 Scorecard

5–15 weekly numbers that give a true pulse on the business. Every number has an owner and a weekly goal. Green = on track, Red = issue. The scorecard means leadership never gets surprised — problems surface fast.

PHG scorecard likely includes: Weekly sales per location, labor %, cover counts, online review scores, open issues count, system uptime. Ask Bill what's on theirs.
▾
What a good Scorecard metric looks like
→ Weekly. If it's monthly, it's a report, not a scorecard.
→ One owner. A metric owned by everyone is owned by no one.
→ A weekly goal. Not a range — a specific number that is either hit or missed.
→ Leading, not lagging. Ideally catches problems before they show up in P&L.
Tech-specific metrics that might land on PHG's scorecard
→ Open IT support tickets / avg resolution time
→ POS uptime % across locations
→ Digital order volume vs. in-person
→ System-related comps or voids (often a POS config signal)
🗂 V/TO — Vision/Traction Organizer

The one-page strategic plan for the entire company. Two sides: Vision (where we're going) and Traction (how we're getting there this year and this quarter). Every employee should see where the company is headed.

Key ask for Bill: "Can you walk me through PHG's V/TO?" Understanding their 10-year target and 3-year picture will tell you everything about what they need from this role.
▾
The 8 sections of the V/TO
Core Values
Who we are. Non-negotiable behaviors.
Core Focus
Purpose/cause/passion + our niche.
10-Year Target
The big, audacious goal.
Marketing Strategy
Target market, 3 uniques, guarantee.
3-Year Picture
What does the company look like in 3 years?
1-Year Plan
Revenue goal + 3–7 priorities for the year.
Rocks
This quarter's 3–7 priorities.
Issues List
Long-term issues parking lot.
🔗 Integrator vs. Visionary

From Rocket Fuel. The Visionary is the founder/owner — big ideas, relationships, culture. The Integrator runs the day-to-day: manages the team, resolves issues, executes the plan. Most companies have a Visionary but need a strong Integrator.

For Jimmy: Bill has been the Integrator for tech. The Director of Business Systems role is essentially becoming the tech Integrator. Understanding this framing matters.
▾
🌟 Visionary (Bill / Ownership)
→ Big picture thinking
→ New ideas, new opportunities
→ Relationships and culture
→ Problem creation (sometimes!)
→ The "why" and the destination
⚙ Integrator (Jimmy's role)
→ Executes the vision
→ Manages the team day-to-day
→ Resolves conflicts and issues
→ Holds people accountable
→ The "how" and the follow-through
The Integrator is the glue. When the Visionary has 10 ideas a day, the Integrator filters them, prioritizes, and executes the right ones. That's the job.
🧩 Accountability Chart

Not a traditional org chart. It defines the major functions of the business (not titles), who owns each function, and what they're accountable for. Every seat has a clear owner with 3–5 key accountabilities.

Ask Bill: Where does the Director of Business Systems sit on the Accountability Chart? What are the 3–5 accountabilities of the seat?
▾
How it's different from an org chart
→ Organized by function, not title. "Sales & Marketing" is a seat, not "VP of Marketing."
→ One person per seat. No shared ownership. Shared accountability = no accountability.
→ Each seat has 3–5 accountabilities. The things that person alone is responsible for delivering.
→ One person can sit in multiple seats in a small company — but it's explicit, not assumed.
Likely accountabilities for Director of Business Systems at PHG
→ All technology systems operational and current
→ Vendor relationships for tech platforms
→ Technology training and documentation for operations
→ Systems integrations and data integrity
→ Tech roadmap aligned with PHG's V/TO
